Executive Summary

  • Historical Injury: Miami's Overtown neighborhood — once known as "The Harlem of the South" — was destroyed by 1960s urban renewal, displacing 40,000+ Black residents for I-95 highway construction.
  • Current Distress: Miami-Dade County has the highest concentration of Black poverty in Florida, with significant disparities in housing, health, and education.
  • Legal Opening: Florida's 2025 Schools of Hope laws (HB 443, HB 1105, HB 1255, SB 2510) give charter schools — and by extension Black communities — right of first refusal on surplus school properties.
  • Property Pipeline: FLDOE publishes annual Vacant & Underused Facilities Reports for all 67 counties, identifying specific properties available for reuse.

Historical Foundation — Overtown & Black Miami

Overtown was Miami's historic Black cultural and economic hub, home to theaters, hotels, churches, and businesses that formed the backbone of the city's Black community. In the 1960s, urban renewal programs destroyed over 40 blocks of Overtown to make way for Interstate 95, displacing approximately 40,000 Black residents and dismantling a thriving community.

Ralph C. McCartney — An original Overtown resident who was interviewed in 1997 as part of a University of Florida oral history project documenting the neighborhood's legacy. His testimony, along with dozens of others, preserves the memory of what was lost and the resilience of those who survived the displacement.1

Today, the Greater Miami area still bears the scars of this history. Black Americans in Miami-Dade experience:

  • Poverty rates of approximately 25-30% compared to ~12% for white residents2
  • Median household income roughly 40% of white median income
  • Significantly higher rates of housing cost burden (>50% of income)
  • Disparities in educational outcomes, health indicators, and economic mobility

HUD Section 3 — Federal Framework for Economic Opportunity

HUD Section 3 (12 U.S.C. § 1701u) requires that recipients of certain HUD funding provide employment, training, and contracting opportunities to low-income residents in connection with assisted projects. In Miami-Dade County, this applies to activities of the Miami-Dade Housing Authority and other HUD grantees.

Key Data Points

  • Miami-Dade County receives approximately $40M+ annually in HUD funding (CDBG, HOME, Housing Choice Vouchers)
  • Section 3 applies to Public Housing Authority capital fund expenditures over $200,000
  • Recipients must demonstrate that 30% of new hires are Section 3 residents
  • Contracting goals require 10% of construction contracts to go to Section 3 businesses

Legal Framework — Schools of Hope (2025)

During the 2025 Florida legislative session, four bills were passed that collectively create the most favorable legal environment for charter school expansion — and by extension Black community property reclamation — in the state's history.

HB 443 Charter School Administration and Operations (Ch. 2025-144)

  • Public Facility Status: Clarifies that charter schools are public schools and must be treated as a public facility for concurrency related to community development.
  • Right of First Refusal: Before a school district can sell, transfer, or lease any real property (including school facilities), it must provide written notice to every charter school in the district. Charter schools receive a right of first refusal to purchase, lease, or use the property for educational purposes.
  • Ad Valorem Exemption: Any facility used to house an approved charter school is exempt from ad valorem taxes (pursuant to s. 196.1983).
  • Existing Facility Reuse: Libraries, museums, performing arts centers, churches, universities, and other public institutional facilities can provide space to charter schools without rezoning, special exceptions, or land use changes — under their preexisting zoning designations.
  • Regulatory Parity: Interlocal agreements or ordinances that impose greater regulatory burden on charter schools than on traditional school districts are void and unenforceable.

HB 1105 Education Omnibus Bill (Ch. 2025-109)

  • Parent-Initiated Conversion: Allows a majority of parents/guardians to convert a district-run public school to a public charter school without input from school teachers or administrators.
  • Job Engine Charter Schools: Authorizes municipalities to apply to convert existing public schools into "job engine charter schools" — workforce-focused charter institutions.
  • Surtax Revenue Sharing: Requires school districts to share discretionary surtax (infrastructure) revenue with eligible charter schools based on enrollment, for levies approved on or after July 1, 2025.

HB 1255 Charter School Local Regulation Preemption

  • Land Use Preemption: Local governments cannot enforce building, site, or operational rules on charter schools (parking, student capacity, hours of operation, site size) unless those rules are uniformly applied to all public schools in the area.
  • Exemption from Rezoning: Charter schools are exempt from land use changes or permits that would not be required of other public or private schools at the same location.

SB 2510 Education Appropriations — Schools of Hope

  • Funding Floor: Requires Schools of Hope program funds to be maintained at $25 million or more annually, starting July 1, 2027.
  • Expanded Definition: "Persistently low-performing school" now includes schools in the bottom 10% in at least two of three years for grade 3 ELA or grade 4 mathematics.

Property Inventory — Vacant & Underused Facilities

The Florida Department of Education publishes annual Vacant & Underused Facilities Reports for all 67 counties. These reports identify closed, vacant, and underutilized school properties — the exact inventory that HB 443's right-of-first-refusal and HB 1105's conversion pathways are designed to channel to charter operators and communities.

These are your target properties. Each PDF contains specific addresses, facility conditions, and usage status. Combined with the legal framework above, these reports represent a pipeline for property acquisition and community redevelopment.

Community Voices & Oral History

The destruction of Overtown and its aftermath has been documented through several oral history projects that preserve the voices of those who lived through it:

University of Florida African American History Project (AAHP)

  • Over 600+ oral history interviews conducted with African Americans throughout Florida
  • One of the largest public African American history collections in the United States
  • Includes interviews with Miami-Dade residents, including original Overtown community members
  • Ralph C. McCartney Interview (1997): An original Overtown resident interviewed as part of a UF project documenting the neighborhood's legacy and the community's experience of displacement.1

Samuel Proctor Oral History Program (SPOHP)

  • Based at the University of Florida, College of Liberal Arts and Sciences
  • Hosts the African American History Project and the "Reanimating African American Histories of the Gulf South" initiative
  • Over 25 public history programs and community-based oral history workshops across Florida

Other Oral History Resources

  • HistoryMiami Museum — Archives of Miami-Dade Black history
  • Florida Memory Project (floridamemory.com) — State archives including Black oral histories
  • Black Archives at Florida A&M University — Research collection on Black Florida history

Strategic Recommendations — A Reparative Justice Framework

1. Property Reclamation Pipeline

  • Obtain the current Vacant & Underused Facilities Report for each priority county
  • Cross-reference with historical Black neighborhood boundaries (Overtown, Coconut Grove, Richmond Heights, etc.)
  • Identify properties in or adjacent to historically displaced communities
  • Exercise right of first refusal under HB 443 before properties hit the open market

2. Community Engagement Strategy

  • File public records requests for all surplus property disposition notices in target counties
  • Build relationships with school board members to receive early notice of property decisions
  • Organize community coalitions to support charter applications for reclaimed properties
  • Use parent-initiated conversion (HB 1105) where existing schools serve Black communities

3. Legal & Funding Leverage

  • Apply for Schools of Hope funding ($25M+ annual pool) to support charter development on reclaimed properties
  • Challenge any local zoning or regulatory barriers under HB 1255's preemption provisions
  • Ensure HUD Section 3 compliance for any housing-adjacent property development
  • Pursue ad valorem tax exemptions (HB 443) for charter school facilities

4. Reparative Justice Framework

The Core Principle: The destruction of Overtown was not just a planning decision — it was the deliberate dismantling of Black wealth, community, and self-determination. Florida's current legal framework creates a pathway for partial repair: returning surplus public property to community-controlled institutions (charter schools) that serve the descendants of those displaced. This is not charity — it is the return of what was taken, enabled by law.
