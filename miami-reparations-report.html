1. Executive Summary

  • Historical Injury: Miami's Overtown neighborhood — "The Harlem of the South" — was destroyed by 1960s urban renewal, displacing 40,000+ Black residents for I-95 highway construction. The community's churches, businesses, and homes were erased in the name of "progress."
  • Current Distress: Miami-Dade County has the highest concentration of Black poverty in Florida. Across 4 priority counties, Black residents experience 2-2.5x the poverty rate, significantly lower homeownership, higher housing cost burden, and persistent health and education gaps.
  • Legal Opening: Florida's 2025 Schools of Hope laws (HB 443, HB 1105, HB 1255, SB 2510) create an unprecedented charter school property pipeline — including right of first refusal on surplus school properties — that Black communities can leverage for reclamation.
  • Community Voices: Over 600 oral history interviews in UF's African American History Project preserve the memories of those who lived through displacement, including Ralph C. McCartney (1997), an original Overtown resident.

2. Historical Foundation — Overtown & Black Miami

2.1 Overtown: "The Harlem of the South"

Overtown was Miami's historic Black cultural and economic hub, centered around NW 2nd Avenue and NW 6th Street. At its peak in the 1940s-1950s, it featured:

  • The Lyric Theater (1913) — one of the first Black-owned theaters in the Southeast
  • St. John's Baptist Church — spiritual and civic anchor of the community
  • Mary Elizabeth Hotel — hosted Black entertainers refused entry to white hotels (Count Basie, Ella Fitzgerald, Cab Calloway)
  • Over 300 Black-owned businesses — retail, professional services, restaurants, and entertainment venues
  • Population: ~40,000 residents in a 20-block area, entirely self-sustaining under Jim Crow segregation

2.2 Destruction: Urban Renewal & I-95

Between 1956 and 1970, federal urban renewal programs and the construction of Interstate 95 destroyed the core of Overtown:

  • 1956: Federal Housing Administration designated Overtown as "blighted" — enabling eminent domain seizure
  • 1960s: Construction of I-95 and I-395 bisected the neighborhood, destroying 40+ blocks
  • Displacement: ~40,000 Black residents displaced, many to Liberty City, Carol City, and other outlying areas
  • Replacement: The land became the Bayfront Park area, James L. Knight Center, and highway infrastructure
What Was Lost: The destruction of Overtown eliminated an estimated $2.5 billion (in 2024 dollars) in generational Black wealth in Miami. Displaced families received pennies on the seized properties. No reparations were paid. The community's social fabric — churches, civic organizations, business networks — was permanently fractured.

Ralph C. McCartney Interview (1997)
Ralph C. McCartney is an original Overtown resident interviewed on June 14, 1997, as part of a University of Florida oral history project documenting the neighborhood's legacy. His testimony captures the lived experience of growing up in Overtown before its destruction — the tight-knit community, the institutions, and the eventual displacement. 5. HUD Section 3 — Federal Framework For Economic Opportunity

5.1 What is Section 3?

HUD Section 3 is a federal regulation (24 CFR Part 135) requiring that recipients of certain HUD funding provide economic opportunities to low-income residents in connection with assisted projects. It applies to all projects receiving more than $200,000 in public housing capital, operating, or modernization assistance, as well as community development and housing choice voucher assistance over the applicable thresholds.

5.2 Key Requirements

  • Employment: 30% of new hires on Section 3 projects must be Section 3 residents
  • Contracting: 10% of construction contracts and 3% of all other contracts must go to Section 3 businesses
  • Training: Connect local residents to job training, apprenticeships, and career pathways
  • Reporting: Annual Section 3 reports required from all recipients (form HUD-60002)

5.3 Miami-Dade County Context

Miami-Dade County is one of the largest HUD grantees in Florida, receiving approximately $40M+ annually across CDBG, HOME, HOPWA, and Housing Choice Voucher programs. This funding:

  • Supports public housing for ~20,000+ families
  • Funds affordable development through LIHTC and bond programs
  • Provides supportive services for homeless individuals and families
  • Supports Section 3 job training through Miami Dade College partnerships

5.4 How Section 3 Connects to Reparative Justice

The Legal Link: For surplus school properties being converted to charter schools under HB 443, if any portion of the property's disposition, renovation, or operation involves HUD funding (e.g., CDBG-funded community space, Section 8 housing on-site, or Section 108 loans), Section 3 requirements must be fulfilled. This mandates employment and contracting opportunities for low-income Black residents — creating an economic opportunity pipeline tied to the charter property.

5.5 Key HUD Reports for Miami-Dade

  • Miami-Dade County Consolidated Plan (5-year strategy) — annual updates
  • Public Housing Authority Plans (annual and 5-year)
  • Fair Housing Assessment (Analysis of Impediments to Fair Housing)
  • HOME Annual Performance Report (APR)
  • Section 3 Summary Reports (HUD-60002)
  • Environmental Justice Screening Report (EPA EJSCREEN)

8. Legal Framework — Schools of Hope (2025)

Four bills passed in the 2025 Florida legislative session create the legal infrastructure for charter school property acquisition and community-controlled development.

HB 443 Charter School Administration and Operations (Ch. 2025-144)

  • Public Facility Status: Charter schools are public schools = public facility for community development concurrency
  • Right of First Refusal: School districts must offer surplus properties to charter schools before any other buyer
  • Ad Valorem Exemption: Charter school facilities exempt from property taxes (s. 196.1983)
  • Existing Facility Reuse: Churches, libraries, museums, universities can host charters without rezoning
  • Regulatory Parity: Ordinances imposing greater burden on charters are void and unenforceable

✓ Link verified (200): https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2025/443

HB 1105 Education Omnibus Bill (Ch. 2025-109)

  • Parent-Initiated Conversion: Majority of parents can convert district school to charter without teacher/admin input
  • Job Engine Charter Schools: Municipalities can convert public schools to workforce-focused charters
  • Surtax Revenue Sharing: Districts must share discretionary surtax revenue with charters based on enrollment

✓ Link verified (200): https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2025/1105

HB 1255 Charter School Local Regulation Preemption

  • Land Use Preemption: Local governments cannot impose parking, capacity, hours, or site size rules on charters unless uniformly applied to all public schools
  • Rezoning Exemption: Charters exempt from land use changes not required of other schools at same location

SB 2510 Education Appropriations — Schools of Hope

  • Funding Floor: Schools of Hope program maintained at $25M+ annually starting July 1, 2027
  • Expanded Definition: "Persistently low-performing" includes bottom 10% in grade 3 ELA or grade 4 math

10. Strategic Recommendations — A Reparative Justice Framework

10.1 Property Reclamation Pipeline

  • Obtain current Vacant & Underused Facilities Reports for all 4 priority counties
  • Cross-reference with historical Black neighborhood boundaries (Overtown, Liberty City, Richmond Heights, Coconut Grove)
  • Identify properties in or adjacent to historically displaced communities
  • Exercise right of first refusal under HB 443 before properties reach open market
  • Apply for ad valorem tax exemption on acquired facilities

10.2 Community Engagement Strategy

  • File standing public records requests for all surplus property disposition notices in target counties
  • Build relationships with school board members to receive early notice of property decisions
  • Organize community coalitions to support charter applications for reclaimed properties
  • Use parent-initiated conversion (HB 1105) where existing schools serve Black communities
  • Engage HUD Section 3 requirements for any housing-adjacent property development

10.3 Legal & Funding Leverage

  • Apply for Schools of Hope funding ($25M+ annual pool) to support charter development
  • Challenge any local zoning or regulatory barriers under HB 1255's preemption provisions
  • Ensure Section 3 compliance for CDBG/HOME-funded components of acquired properties
  • Pursue municipal "job engine charter school" designation for workforce-focused institutions

10.4 Reparative Justice Framework

The Core Principle: The destruction of Overtown was not a planning decision — it was the deliberate dismantling of Black wealth, community, and self-determination. Florida's current legal framework creates a pathway for partial repair: returning surplus public property to community-controlled institutions (charter schools) that serve the descendants of those displaced. This is not charity — it is the return of what was taken, enabled by law.
